Message Loupe
Product review and recommendations
Prepared July 10, 2026

From strong detector to essential decision system

Message Loupe already has a credible engine, a trustworthy privacy model, and a calm interface. Its next step is not simply detecting more suspicious signals. It is turning reliable analysis into a repeatable action that prevents expensive mistakes.

Message Loupe should become the approval layer between an email and an irreversible action.
9/10Technical credibility and defensive depth
8/10Core product insight and market wedge
5/10Activation and everyday usability
4/10Compounding product and distribution loop

What should be protected

  • The central insight is right: authenticated delivery does not make a payment or credential request safe.
  • Privacy is real architecture: browser-side parsing and explicit rules make the promise verifiable.
  • The explanations are inspectable: users can understand why a verdict was reached without accepting an opaque score.
  • The visual tone fits the task: calm, precise, and free of fear-driven security imagery.
  • The validation discipline is serious: 153 committed tests pass, and 52 private known-fake samples currently produce zero Safe verdicts and zero analysis errors.

Overall judgment: this is substantially better than a polished prototype. The engine has enough depth to support a real product. The larger gap is the workflow around that engine.

Priority recommendations

1

Move the capability into the inbox

Saving an .eml, finding raw headers, and leaving the inbox creates too much friction during a stressful decision. Gmail and Outlook integration is the actual product surface, not a secondary enhancement.

Until an add-in ships, place the live scanner in the primary hero position and move the sample result and saving instructions below it.

Evidence: the homepage teaches users how to obtain an original email before it presents the scanner. The integration roadmap already identifies inbox add-ins as the paid wedge.

2

Make the result action-first

Safe, Caution, and Likely fake describe evidence. The user needs an immediate decision:

  • Continue normally
  • Verify outside the email
  • Stop and escalate

Add a decision and recommended-action layer that is independent of the path used to calculate the verdict. Money, credential, payroll, and bank-detail requests should always surface the appropriate outside-verification action.

Evidence: the prominent phone-verification alert currently depends on an internal capped flag. A message can contain wire-transfer risk and receive Caution through another signal without displaying that alert.

3

Retire the public “Safe” claim

The interface currently explains that “Safe means authentic.” That is stronger than the underlying evidence supports. Domain authentication cannot establish who controls a mailbox or whether the requested action is honest.

Keep the internal enum if convenient, but use a public label such as No obvious warning signs or Delivery appears authentic. Continue stating that high-risk actions require verification outside the email.

4

Make every explanation correspond to the exact signal

A test message containing “updated bank details” correctly received Caution, but the broad document-request detector caused the result to claim that passports, IDs, or certificates had been requested.

Split the current classification into personal documents, bank details, payment instructions, secure-document portals, and employment paperwork. A correct verdict with an incorrect explanation still damages trust.

5

Add a hard legitimate-email benchmark

The private known-fake corpus provides an excellent false-negative gate. It does not measure how often legitimate invoices, HR messages, bank notices, role-based senders, and payment discussions become noisy cautions.

Every detector family should have known-fake examples, ordinary legitimate examples, and legitimate edge cases containing the same vocabulary. Track both missed scams and unnecessary cautions.

6

Separate shipped value from future roadmap copy

The consumer homepage currently describes pilot workflows, prototype add-ins, and future team records. Replace that section with benefits available now. Introduce a business call to action only when there is a concrete design-partner offer.

The hidden business page contains sound internal strategy, but phrases such as “The buyer is not a consumer” and “Pilot pricing hypothesis” should be rewritten before publication.

7

Correct the mobile scanner toolbar and mode semantics

At a mobile test width, the page expanded from a 375px content viewport to 436px because the save-help control was forced alongside the upload and paste controls. Stack or wrap that toolbar at narrow widths.

Implement the mode chooser as accessible tabs or pressed-state buttons with visible keyboard focus and an announced selected state.

The bolder product thesis

Build a focused pre-action control for small finance teams. The first version should address one expensive workflow: a payment or bank-detail change received by email.

Payment Change Firewall

  1. An employee opens a message in Gmail or Outlook.
  2. Message Loupe identifies the action being requested, then analyzes the sender, route, content, links, and attachments.
  3. The organization’s policy determines the required response.
  4. A payment or bank-detail change requires a known-number callback and, when configured, a second approver.
  5. The employee records the outcome without storing the raw message.
  6. Message Loupe creates a privacy-preserving receipt containing the verdict, reason codes, verifier, policy followed, and completion time.

This changes the value proposition from “a better phishing checker” to a controlled process that prevents an unauthorized payment and proves that verification occurred.

How the product becomes accretive

  • The free scanner earns trust: useful public checks, methodology, and search content create credibility.
  • The add-in creates habit: the product becomes available at the moment of uncertainty without requiring file handling.
  • Verification receipts create invitations: a user shares a result with an approver, bringing a colleague into the workflow.
  • Organization policies create retention: bank changes, payroll requests, credentials, and documents each gain a defined response.
  • Explicit feedback improves calibration: users can report a wrong explanation or missed risk by sending only the signal and verdict metadata they consent to share.
  • Industry policy packs create distribution: bookkeeping, legal, real-estate, and family-office practices can adopt templates matched to their recurring risks.

Recommended first buyer: outsourced bookkeepers and small accounting firms. They encounter payment-risk email repeatedly across multiple clients, which should produce greater usage frequency and a natural route into additional organizations.

Recommended 90-day sequence

TimingProduct workEvidence of success
Weeks 1–2 Fix mobile overflow, scanner semantics, misleading explanations, the Safe label, and action-alert logic. Add a known-good calibration suite. All checks pass. No known-fake email returns Safe. Legitimate calibration cases stay within an agreed caution rate.
Weeks 2–4 Recruit three to five bookkeeping or finance-team design partners. Observe the exact steps they use for payment changes. At least three teams agree to run a real pilot and identify a person responsible for payment verification.
Weeks 4–8 Ship one inbox integration selected by the pilot customers. Implement the decision and recommended-action model. Users can check the open message and reach an actionable result without saving a file or finding headers.
Weeks 8–12 Add payment-change policy, verification completion, a second-approver option, and privacy-preserving receipts. Teams complete real verification workflows and can review open versus completed follow-ups without storing message bodies.

Build now, defer deliberately

Build now

  • Action-oriented verdicts
  • Balanced detector calibration
  • One inbox integration
  • Payment-change verification
  • Metadata-only receipts
  • A small design-partner pilot

Defer

  • A broad administrative dashboard
  • Two add-ins developed simultaneously
  • Opaque remote AI classification
  • Decorative product analytics
  • Dozens of additional signals without calibration
  • Public claims for unshipped team features
